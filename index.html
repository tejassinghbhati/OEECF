01
The Structural Silo Problem

Epidemiological models — SIR, SEIR and their extensions — produce time-series of infection prevalence, hospitalisation burden, and mortality rates. Macroeconomic models — OG-Core, CGE frameworks — operate over labour endowments, total factor productivity, and aggregate demand. These two scientific communities have historically operated in structural isolation.

During public health crises, policymakers require real-time feedback loops between disease trajectories and economic output. The absence of a formal translation layer forces analysts to rely on ad-hoc, non-reproducible conversions — typically scalar assumptions applied uniformly to economic parameters without rigorous epidemiological grounding.

OEECF resolves this by acting as a soft-linking middleware: it does not permanently couple the two model classes, but systematically translates outputs from the epidemiological layer into mathematically rigorous inputs for any downstream macroeconomic solver.

02
Soft-Linking Coupling Architecture
Coupling Pipeline · Data Flow
EpiData
Pydantic-validated time-series schema. Accepts SIR or SEIR output: S(t), I(t), R(t), H(t), Q(t), D(t) as normalised population fractions.
EpiEconCoupler
Core orchestrator. Binds EconParameters, dispatches to three independent shock calculators, returns a validated MacroShocks object.
MacroShocks
Output container holding three time-indexed multiplier vectors: labour supply L(t), total factor productivity Z(t), and demand D(t).
Translators
OGCoreTranslator serialises to JSON with labour_endowment_multiplier and Z scalars. CGETranslator converts to percentage-change Δ% deltas.
03
Macroeconomic Shock Derivation
03.1  ·  LSS  ·  Labour Supply Shock
Labour Supply Multiplier
L(t) = max( 0,  1 − H(t) − D(t) − α · I(t) − β · Q(t) )

Quantifies the fraction of the effective labour force that remains operational at each time step. Workers exit the productive supply via hospitalisation, death, or critical illness. Quarantined workers are partially recovered through the economy's remote-work capacity.

When hospitalisation H(t) exceeds the system's healthcare capacity threshold (default 5 % of population), an overflow fatality multiplier of 3.0× compounds mortality — cascading additional labour losses that accelerate the shock at epidemic peak.

D(t) Deceased (cumulative) fraction —
α · I(t) Infectious workers, sick-at-work factor α = 0.90
β · Q(t) Quarantined, remote-work adjusted loss β = 0.65

03.2  ·  TFP  ·  Total Factor Productivity Shock
Productivity Multiplier
Z(t) = [ Σ wi(t)  ·  pi  /  W ] − μ · I(t)

Models aggregate productivity loss by segmenting the workforce into three operational cohorts: normal on-site workers (100 % productivity), remote quarantined workers (80 % efficiency), and sick workers still attending (10 % productivity).

A macro-friction penalty μ · I(t) captures supply-chain disruption, input delivery failures, and coordination overhead that depresses TFP independently of direct workforce availability — proportional to the infectious fraction at each time step.

Component Variables
wN Normal workers × participation rate p = 1.00
wR Remote workers × participation rate η = 0.80
wS Sick workers × participation rate p = 0.10
μ · I(t) Macro-friction penalty, supply-chain μ = 0.50
Simulation output · 180 days · SIR (β=0.30, γ=0.10)

03.3  ·  DS  ·  Demand Shock
Consumption Demand Multiplier
D(t) = max( 0.20,  1 − φ · I(t) − 0.8 · Q(t) )

Captures the contraction in aggregate consumption demand driven by two distinct mechanisms: infection-induced behavioural fear — voluntary reduction in activity by the infectious population — and quarantine-mandated suppression, which mechanically limits consumption capacity.

A minimum subsistence floor of 0.20 bounds the multiplier, reflecting the irreducible essential consumption that persists even under complete quarantine — food, healthcare, utilities. The floor prevents economically implausible collapse scenarios in severe epidemic simulations.

Component Variables
φ · I(t) Fear-factor demand suppression φ = 0.50
0.8 · Q(t) Quarantine consumption suppression fixed
floor Minimum subsistence consumption 0.20
Simulation output · 180 days · SIR (β=0.30, γ=0.10)
